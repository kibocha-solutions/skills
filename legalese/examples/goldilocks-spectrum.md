## How to Use This File

Each example begins with a **layman input** — the kind of plain instruction a user might provide. Below it, all four tiers show how that input is realised at each register. The annotations explain the structural and vocabulary decisions at each tier.

The tiers are graduated, not interchangeable. Each tier builds on the one below it:

- **Standard** prioritises plain-language clarity. A non-lawyer should understand the output.
- **Formal** adds professional legal structure and defined terms. A business reader should be comfortable.
- **Sovereign** adds classical authority, sovereign granting, and foundational instrument language. The current Goldilocks target.
- **Archaic** pushes into near-historical reconstruction. Comprehensibility to a legal-English reader, not a layperson.

## The Repugnancy Clause

**Layman input:** "Any rule that goes against this Constitution is canceled."

---

### Standard

> If any law, regulation, or rule conflicts with this Constitution, that law, regulation, or rule does not apply to the extent of the conflict. The rest of it remains in effect.

**What this targets:** Maximum accessibility. "Does not apply" replaces all classical termination language. "To the extent of the conflict" scopes the invalidity. "The rest of it remains in effect" is partial severability in plain terms. No Latin, no archaic framing.

---

### Formal

> To the extent that any law, regulation, or other instrument conflicts with this Constitution, such instrument shall be of no force or effect with respect to the inconsistent provisions. All provisions of such instrument that are not inconsistent with this Constitution shall continue in full force and effect.

**What this targets:** Professional legal register. "Shall be of no force or effect" is the standard Formal termination phrase. "With respect to the inconsistent provisions" scopes with precision. The instrument uses "shall" for obligations and "such instrument" for formal reference. No notwithstanding frame — Formal reserves that for explicit conflict-resolution clauses between identified instruments.

---

### Sovereign

> Notwithstanding any custom or decree to the contrary, where any instrument subsists in repugnancy to this Constitution, such instrument shall perish to the extent of its inconsistency, and be rendered void ab initio.

**What this targets:** The Goldilocks standard. The notwithstanding opener forecloses all prior law and custom. "Subsists in repugnancy" establishes the trigger with classical precision. "Perish to the extent of its inconsistency" calibrates the scope. "Void ab initio" closes with retroactive nullity. Every element is comprehensible to a modern reader without glossing.

---

### Archaic

> Whatsoever statute, decree, writ, or instrument doth subsist in repugnancy to this Constitution, the same is hereby declared to be holden for naught and utterly void, as though it had never been enacted, proclaimed, or conceived; and no power, authority, or instrument shall avail to restore it, notwithstanding any custom, usage, or prior enactment to the contrary.

**What this targets:** Near-historical reconstruction. "Doth subsist" mirrors historical verb conjugation. "Holden for naught" echoes medieval nullification language. "As though it had never been enacted, proclaimed, or conceived" is an extended retroactive denial covering three distinct vectors of legal creation. The notwithstanding frame is moved to the end in the classical English Bill of Rights pattern. The sentence is long and ornate, but every element carries legal load.

## The Unamendable Provision

**Layman input:** "This Article cannot be changed by anyone at any time."

---

### Standard

> This Article may not be amended, repealed, or modified. No person or body has the authority to change it.

**What this targets:** Direct prohibition in plain language. "Amended, repealed, or modified" is required enumeration — each word targets a distinct vector (formal change, removal, alteration). "No person or body" closes both individual and institutional vectors.

---

### Formal

> This Article shall not be subject to amendment, repeal, or modification by any person, body, or instrument. Any purported amendment of this Article is null and void.

**What this targets:** Professional prohibition with a consequence clause. "Shall not be subject to" is the Formal obligation form. "Any purported amendment" addresses the attempt itself. "Null and void" is the Formal termination phrase. Two sentences, two commands: the prohibition and its consequence.

---

### Sovereign

> This Article is the sole unamendable provision of this Constitution, and shall subsist in perpetuity, inviolate, beyond the reach of any amending power, instrument, or decree.

**What this targets:** Sovereign entrenchment. "Shall subsist in perpetuity" establishes temporal permanence. "Inviolate" closes the protection vector. "Beyond the reach of any amending power, instrument, or decree" forecloses three distinct threat vectors: the constitutional capacity to amend, any legal instrument, any specific authoritative command.

---

### Archaic

> This Article is the sole unamendable provision of this Constitution, and shall subsist in perpetuity, inviolate and beyond the reach of any amending power, instrument, decree, or resolution whatsoever. Any instrument, howsoever enacted, that doth purport to amend, abridge, or abrogate this Article is hereby declared null from inception and stillborn upon its face, and no authority under heaven shall avail to give it force or recognition.

**What this targets:** Maximum sovereign entrenchment with archaic force. "Whatsoever" and "howsoever" are archaic intensifiers. "Doth purport" mirrors historical conjugation. "Null from inception and stillborn upon its face" deploys the paired termination phrases (temporal + facial vectors). "No authority under heaven" is a rhetorical absolute that echoes historical proclamation. The construction is long but each element earns its place.

## The Nullification Mechanism

**Layman input:** "If an instrument is found to violate this Article, it will be struck down."

---

### Standard

> If an instrument is found to conflict with this Article, or is reasonably likely to produce an outcome that this Article prohibits, the conflicting parts of that instrument do not apply. Parts of the instrument that do not conflict with this Article remain in effect.

**What this targets:** Two-part trigger (actual conflict + reasonable likelihood) in accessible language. Partial scoping with plain severability. No Latin, no archaic phrasing.

---

### Formal

> Where an instrument is determined by a court of competent jurisdiction to be inconsistent with this Article, or where such instrument is reasonably likely to produce an outcome prohibited by this Article, the inconsistent provisions of such instrument shall be of no force or effect. Provisions of such instrument that are not inconsistent with this Article shall continue in full force and effect.

**What this targets:** Formal legal register with institutional mechanics. "Determined by a court of competent jurisdiction" specifies the adjudicating body. The two-part trigger is retained. "Shall be of no force or effect" is the Formal termination phrase.

---

### Sovereign

> Where an instrument produces, or is reasonably likely to produce, an outcome that this Article finds intolerable, the instrument responsible shall be nullified to the extent its operation is repugnant to this Article, and no further.

**What this targets:** Sovereign precision. "Finds intolerable" positions the Article as the sovereign arbiter. "Nullified to the extent its operation is repugnant" scopes the invalidation precisely. "And no further" is the economy constraint — no broader invalidation than necessary.

---

### Archaic

> Where any instrument doth produce, or is reasonably to be expected to produce, an outcome that this Article holdeth intolerable, the instrument responsible shall be nullified and rendered void to the full extent of its repugnancy to this Article, and no further; and no court, tribunal, or authority shall give force or recognition to the offending provisions, which shall be holden for naught from the moment of their enactment.

**What this targets:** Extended archaic construction. "Doth produce" and "holdeth intolerable" use historical conjugation. "Holden for naught from the moment of their enactment" is archaic retroactive nullification. The prohibition on courts giving force is an explicit foreclosure clause that echoes the English Bill of Rights pattern.

## The Prohibition on Recognition

**Layman input:** "No one can enforce an outcome that is not listed in this Article."

---

### Standard

> No person may recognise, enforce, or give effect to any outcome that is not expressly listed in this Article. Any attempt to do so has no legal effect.

**What this targets:** Plain prohibition with three distinct verbs (recognise, enforce, give effect to) targeting different moments in the enforcement chain. The consequence is stated in modern terms.

---

### Formal

> No person shall recognise, declare, enforce, or give effect to any outcome not expressly enumerated within this Article. Any purported recognition or enforcement of such an unenumerated outcome shall be of no force or effect.

**What this targets:** Formal prohibition with four enumerated verbs. "Expressly enumerated" replaces "listed" for legal precision. "Purported" addresses the attempt. "Shall be of no force or effect" is the Formal termination phrase.

---

### Sovereign

> No person shall recognise, declare, enforce, or give effect to any intolerable outcome not expressly enumerated within this Article; and any purported recognition of such unenumerated outcome shall not stand.

**What this targets:** Sovereign prohibition. The four verbs are retained — each targets a distinct vector in the enforcement chain (acknowledge, pronounce formally, compel compliance, operationalise). "Shall not stand" is the Sovereign termination phrase deployed at its strongest — prospective rejection of any attempt.

---

### Archaic

> No person, body, court, or authority whatsoever shall recognise, declare, enforce, or give effect to any intolerable outcome not expressly enumerated within this Article; and any purported recognition, declaration, or enforcement of such unenumerated outcome is hereby declared to be of no force, validity, or standing, and shall be holden for naught as though it had never been attempted.

**What this targets:** Maximum archaic prohibition. "Whatsoever" intensifies the scope. The prohibition subject is extended to four distinct actors (person, body, court, authority). The consequence clause uses triple enumeration of what is denied (force, validity, standing) — each carries a distinct vector. "Holden for naught as though it had never been attempted" adds retroactive erasure of the attempt itself.

## The Court Jurisdiction Clause

**Layman input:** "Only Clause 4 can be used by courts to cancel instruments under this Article. No other legal principles apply."

---

### Standard

> A court may only invalidate an instrument under this Article using the process set out in Clause 4. No other legal principle, doctrine, or standard may be used to supplement, bypass, or override that process.

**What this targets:** Plain exclusivity with three specific threat vectors (supplement, bypass, override). The reader knows exactly what is excluded and what the sole process is.

---

### Formal

> The jurisdiction to invalidate any instrument under this Article vests exclusively in the mechanism established by Clause 4. A court of competent jurisdiction exercising such power shall derive its authority solely from Clause 4 and from no other source, doctrine, or decree.

**What this targets:** Formal exclusivity with institutional language. "Vests exclusively" is the Formal grant form. "Derive its authority solely from" establishes the single source of power. Three exclusion vectors (source, doctrine, decree).

---

### Sovereign

> The jurisdiction to nullify any instrument under this Article subsists as sovereign and indefeasible, vesting exclusively in the mechanism established by Clause 4. A Court of competent jurisdiction, in exercise of such power, shall derive its authority solely from Clause 4 of this Article and from no other source, doctrine, or decree; and shall brook no doctrine of equity, precedent of common law, or external legal standard that purports to supplement, circumvent, or supersede the sovereign mechanism vested in this Article.

**What this targets:** Full sovereign exclusivity. "Subsists as sovereign and indefeasible" declares the jurisdiction's character. "Brook no" carries classical sovereign impatience. Three doctrinal exclusions (equity, common law precedent, external standard) and three threat vectors (supplement, circumvent, supersede).

---

### Archaic

> The jurisdiction to nullify any instrument under this Article subsists as sovereign, indefeasible, and absolute, vesting exclusively in the mechanism established by Clause 4, and in no other power, authority, or body whatsoever. A Court of competent jurisdiction, in exercise of such power, shall derive its authority solely from Clause 4 of this Article; and shall brook no doctrine of equity, precedent of common law, canon of construction, or external legal standard, howsoever venerable or anciently established, that doth purport to supplement, circumvent, or supersede the sovereign mechanism vested in this Article. Any judgment, order, or decree rendered in derogation of this exclusive jurisdiction is hereby declared void and of no standing, as though it had never been pronounced.

**What this targets:** Maximum archaic exclusivity. "Howsoever venerable or anciently established" forecloses the argument that long-standing precedent can override the clause. "Doth purport" mirrors historical conjugation. "Canon of construction" adds a fourth doctrinal exclusion vector. The final sentence adds a consequence clause that retroactively nullifies any defiant judgment.
