# Termination Phrase Library

Each phrase below may appear at most once within the same instrument. Select the phrase whose legal character best matches the clause being closed. Do not repeat any phrase across the instrument.

The library is divided into two sections: classical phrases (Sovereign and Archaic tiers) and modern equivalents (Standard and Formal tiers). The one-per-instrument distribution rule applies across both sections — do not use a classical phrase and its modern equivalent in the same instrument.

---

## Classical Phrases

Use these at the **Sovereign** and **Archaic** tiers. At the Formal tier, use only where the instrument's context specifically demands classical finality (rare). Do not use at the Standard tier.

---

### void ab initio

**Tier:** Sovereign, Archaic

**Meaning:** The instrument or provision is null from the beginning, as though it never possessed legal force, operation, or apparent validity.

**Legal character:** Technical, retroactive, and foundational. The most precise term of art for total retroactive nullity. Draws on Roman law and is well-established across common law jurisdictions.

**Best deployment:** The first or most foundational nullification clause in the instrument, typically the supremacy or repugnancy clause. Establishes the baseline concept of retroactive nullity that the instrument's other termination phrases presuppose.

**Do not use:** Where nullity is prospective only; where the clause addresses partial rather than total voidance; or where the phrase has already appeared elsewhere in the instrument.

---

### shall not stand

**Tier:** Sovereign, Archaic

**Meaning:** The instrument, act, or purported recognition cannot be maintained, upheld, or given effect.

**Legal character:** Active sovereign rejection. Commands finality without retroactive analysis. The phrase denies prospective operation rather than retroactive existence.

**Best deployment:** Clauses that prohibit recognition or enforcement of unenumerated or unauthorised acts. Strongest in provisions that foreclose courts and institutions from giving effect to prohibited outcomes.

**Do not use:** Where total retroactive nullity is needed (use void ab initio or null from inception instead).

---

### shall be excised

**Tier:** Sovereign, Archaic

**Meaning:** The instrument is surgically removed from the legal body, treated as a foreign element that has no place within the document's closed ecosystem.

**Legal character:** Precise and clinical. The imagery of excision implies the instrument is an unauthorised intrusion that the sovereign document actively expels, rather than one that simply fails to take effect.

**Best deployment:** Unamendability and entrenchment provisions, where an instrument purporting to modify or abrogate the sovereign document must be cut from the legal order without remainder.

**Do not use:** Where the voidance is partial or scoped; the excision phrase implies total removal.

---

### null from inception

**Tier:** Sovereign, Archaic

**Meaning:** The instrument never possessed legitimate legal existence from the moment of its creation.

**Legal character:** Existential retroactivity. Stronger in metaphorical force than void ab initio because it denies any moment of legitimate existence rather than merely declaring legal nullity from the beginning. The emphasis is on the instrument's origin, not its legal classification.

**Best deployment:** Clauses addressing instruments that exist solely or predominantly to produce an intolerable or prohibited outcome; instruments whose formation was fundamentally defective, not merely their operation.

**Pairing note:** Often deployed together with *stillborn upon its face* because their vectors are distinct: null from inception addresses the temporal dimension (when nullity commenced); stillborn upon its face addresses the facial dimension (that no valid purpose was ever present).

---

### stillborn upon its face

**Tier:** Sovereign, Archaic

**Meaning:** The instrument arrived without legal life; its defect was patent at the moment of its creation, apparent on its face to any observer.

**Legal character:** Visceral and absolute. The most forceful denial of legitimacy available in the library. Denies not only legal force but any pretence of legitimate purpose.

**Best deployment:** Paired with *null from inception* in clauses addressing instruments that subsist solely or predominantly to produce a prohibited outcome. The two phrases close complementary vectors and should appear together in the same sub-clause.

**Do not use:** As a standalone phrase where *void ab initio* would be more precise; reserve this phrase for the most extreme cases of facial invalidity.

---

## Modern Equivalents

Use these at the **Standard** and **Formal** tiers. At the Formal tier, classical phrases may occasionally be appropriate where the instrument's context demands it, but default to these modern equivalents. The one-per-instrument distribution rule still applies — do not repeat any phrase.

---

### is void and unenforceable

**Tier:** Standard, Formal

**Meaning:** The provision has no legal force and cannot be enforced by any party.

**Legal character:** Direct and comprehensive. Combines the declaratory ("void") with the practical consequence ("unenforceable"). The most common modern formulation for total invalidity.

**Best deployment:** The primary invalidity clause. Strongest as the first declaration of invalidity in the instrument, establishing the baseline for other provisions.

---

### has no legal effect

**Tier:** Standard, Formal

**Meaning:** The provision produces no legal consequences and creates no rights, obligations, or liabilities.

**Legal character:** Neutral and descriptive. States a fact about the provision's legal status without the forceful connotation of "void." Useful where the tone should be informational rather than commanding.

**Best deployment:** Provisions that address attempted modifications, waivers, or consents that fail to meet formal requirements. The tone communicates that the defective act simply did not register, rather than that it was struck down.

---

### is null and void

**Tier:** Formal (primary), Standard (acceptable)

**Meaning:** The provision is legally nonexistent and without force.

**Legal character:** Traditional commercial formulation. More formal than "is void and unenforceable" but well within the modern register. Familiar to business readers and courts.

**Best deployment:** Breach and termination clauses in commercial instruments. The formulation carries enough weight for contractual consequences without invoking sovereign authority.

---

### shall be of no force or effect

**Tier:** Formal

**Meaning:** The provision carries no operative legal power and produces no consequences.

**Legal character:** Institutional and measured. The "shall be" construction positions the invalidity as a consequence imposed by the instrument, not merely a description. More forceful than "has no legal effect" but less dramatic than classical phrases.

**Best deployment:** Conflict-resolution and hierarchy clauses in master agreements, corporate governance documents, and institutional instruments where the drafting register is professional but not sovereign.

---

### is deemed never to have taken effect

**Tier:** Formal

**Meaning:** The provision is treated retroactively as though it never operated, by legal fiction.

**Legal character:** The modern equivalent of retroactive nullity. The "deemed" construction creates a legal fiction rather than asserting metaphysical retroactive nonexistence (as void ab initio does). Appropriate for commercial and institutional instruments where retroactive treatment is needed without classical Latin.

**Best deployment:** Provisions addressing conditional agreements that fail, options that are not exercised, or approvals that are withdrawn retroactively. The fiction of "deemed never to have taken effect" is the modern functional equivalent of void ab initio.
