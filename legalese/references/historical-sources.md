# Historical Source Reference

This file supports CoLT Step 2. For each foundational instrument, it identifies access paths, relevant sections for sovereign command construction, key vocabulary examples to extract, and the Authority Register tiers at which consulting the source is most valuable.

At the **Sovereign** and **Archaic** tiers, consulting at least one historical source is mandatory before drafting. At the **Formal** tier, consultation is optional and recommended only when the instrument involves hierarchical supremacy or subordination mechanics. At the **Standard** tier, skip historical sources and use the modern drafting references at the end of this file.

---

## Magna Carta (1215)

**Tier relevance:** Sovereign, Archaic

**Access:** Avalon Project at Yale Law School (avalon.law.yale.edu/medieval/magcarta.asp) and the British Library digital archive.

**Relevant sections:** Chapters 29, 39, and 40 for due process and inviolable rights constructions; the opening preamble for sovereign grant language.

**Key formulations:** "No free man shall be seized, imprisoned, dispossessed, outlawed, exiled or ruined in any way..." — exhaustive enumeration of distinct legal vectors with no synonym stacking; "We will not deny or defer to any man either Justice or Right" — absolute negation in the sovereign voice.

**Vocabulary register:** Direct royal command; absolute negation; enumeration of legal domains; no conditional hedging.

---

## Petition of Right (1628)

**Tier relevance:** Sovereign, Archaic

**Access:** Parliament.uk historical records and the Avalon Project at Yale Law School.

**Relevant sections:** The recital clauses for declaratory framing; the final provisions for sovereign constraint language binding the Crown.

**Key formulations:** "Your subjects have inherited this freedom, that they should not be compelled to contribute to any tax, tallage, aid, or other like charge not set by common consent of parliament" — the inherited-right frame; "not be compelled" as sovereign prohibition.

**Vocabulary register:** Declaratory assertion of inherited rights; categorical prohibition; enumeration of taxable categories.

---

## English Bill of Rights (1689)

**Tier relevance:** Sovereign, Archaic

**Access:** Parliament.uk historical records and the Avalon Project at Yale Law School.

**Relevant sections:** The thirteen "whereas" recitals for establishing intolerable prior acts; the declaratory provisions for rights entrenchment.

**Key formulations:** "That the pretended power of suspending the laws or the execution of laws by regal authority without consent of Parliament is illegal" — "pretended power" for dismissing illegitimate authority; present-tense declaration of illegality as a structural pattern.

**Vocabulary register:** Declaratory mode; "pretended power" for instruments without legitimate authority; categorical illegality; the "whereas" recital for framing prior grievances.

---

## Blackstone's Commentaries on the Laws of England (1765-1769)

**Tier relevance:** Formal, Sovereign, Archaic

**Access:** Avalon Project at Yale Law School (avalon.law.yale.edu/subject_menus/blackstone.asp) and Founders Online.

**Relevant sections:** Book I, Chapter 1 (Nature of Laws in General) for the theory of sovereign command; Book I, Chapter 2 for subordination of inferior instruments; Book II, Chapter 1 for sovereign property grants.

**Key formulations:** "Municipal law is a rule of civil conduct prescribed by the supreme power in a state, commanding what is right and prohibiting what is wrong" — the dual command/prohibition structure as the atomic unit of legal authority; "prescribed by the supreme power" for establishing the source of legal force.

**Vocabulary register:** Expository but authoritative; "prescribed by"; "commanding what is right and prohibiting what is wrong"; "supreme power in a state."

**Formal-tier note:** Blackstone's expository style — authoritative without archaic grammar — is a useful calibration point for the Formal tier when the instrument involves institutional hierarchy or the theory of legal authority.

## United States Constitution (1787)

**Tier relevance:** Sovereign, Archaic

**Access:** National Archives (archives.gov) and Constitution.org.

**Relevant sections:** Article VI (Supremacy Clause) for the definitive notwithstanding frame and repugnancy mechanics; Article I Section 9 for categorical prohibition construction; the Preamble for sovereign grant language; the First Amendment for the absolute prohibition form.

**Key formulations:** "This Constitution...shall be the supreme Law of the Land; and the Judges in every State shall be bound thereby, any Thing in the Constitution or Laws of any State to the Contrary notwithstanding" — the notwithstanding supremacy frame in full operation; "Congress shall make no law" — the categorical prohibition with no exceptions or hedging.

**Vocabulary register:** "Shall be the supreme Law"; "to the Contrary notwithstanding"; "Congress shall make no law"; "shall not be infringed"; "shall not be violated."

---

## British North America Act (1867)

**Tier relevance:** Sovereign, Archaic

**Access:** Justice Laws Canada (laws-lois.justice.gc.ca/eng/Const/page-1.html).

**Relevant sections:** Sections 91 and 92 for exclusive distribution of legislative powers; Section 129 for instrument survival and repugnancy mechanics; the Preamble for subordination acknowledgment within a federal system.

**Key formulations:** "All Laws in force in Canada...at the Union...shall continue in Canada and in each Province...until they shall be altered or repealed" — survival mechanics for subordinate instruments; "shall have exclusive Legislative Authority in relation to" — the exclusive sovereign grant form.

**Vocabulary register:** "Shall continue in force"; "until altered or repealed by the Parliament of Canada"; "exclusive Legislative Authority"; "to the extent of such repugnancy."

## Modern Drafting References

Use these at the **Standard** and **Formal** tiers. These are not historical instruments but modern best-practice sources for clear, precise legal drafting.

---

### Plain Language Action and Information Network (PLAIN) Guidelines

**Tier relevance:** Standard (primary), Formal (supplementary)

**Access:** plainlanguage.gov

**Relevant guidance:** Federal Plain Language Guidelines for legal and regulatory writing. Covers sentence structure, word choice, active vs. passive voice, and document organisation.

**Key patterns to extract:** Short sentences with one idea each; active voice as default; defined terms introduced on first use; "if...then" constructions for conditions; avoidance of double negatives and nominalisations.

**Standard-tier note:** This is the primary calibration source for Standard-tier vocabulary and sentence structure. The guidelines are designed for legal and regulatory text that must be accessible to non-specialists.

---

### UK Office of the Parliamentary Counsel — Drafting Guidance

**Tier relevance:** Standard, Formal

**Access:** gov.uk — Office of the Parliamentary Counsel publications.

**Relevant guidance:** Principles for drafting legislation in clear, modern English. Covers the use of "must" vs. "shall," sentence length control, avoidance of archaic language, and structural hierarchy.

**Key patterns to extract:** "Must" for obligations in modern legislative drafting; short sections with descriptive headings; avoidance of "hereby," "hereinafter," "aforementioned"; present tense for statements of law.

---

### UNCITRAL Model Law Patterns

**Tier relevance:** Formal (primary), Standard (supplementary)

**Access:** uncitral.un.org — model laws and legislative guides.

**Relevant guidance:** Structural patterns for international commercial instruments: arbitration clauses, choice-of-law provisions, conflict-resolution hierarchies, and cross-border enforceability mechanics.

**Key patterns to extract:** Neutral, jurisdiction-agnostic phrasing; defined terms with precise scope; hierarchical conflict-resolution ("In the event of a conflict between this Agreement and the applicable Model Law, the Model Law shall prevail"); standard severability formulations.

**Formal-tier note:** UNCITRAL patterns are the strongest calibration source for Formal-tier instruments that operate across jurisdictions or need institutional neutrality.

---

### ABC of Plain Legal Language

**Tier relevance:** Standard

**Access:** Legal writing guides by Michèle Asprey and similar plain-language legal drafting authorities.

**Relevant guidance:** Principles for converting traditional legal language into clear, modern prose while preserving legal precision. Covers elimination of legalese, replacement of archaic constructions, and reader-centred document design.

**Key patterns to extract:** Replace "shall" with "must" or "will"; replace "notwithstanding" with "despite" or "even if"; replace "herein" with "in this agreement"; replace "null and void" with "not valid" or "has no effect" (at Standard tier only — Formal tier retains "null and void").
